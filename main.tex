\title{Characterizing E-Scooter Riding Safety Through City-Scale Speed Profile Analysis}

\author[umn]{Seongjin Choi\corref{cor1}}
\ead{choi@umn.edu}
\cortext[cor1]{Corresponding author}

\address[umn]{Department of Civil, Environmental, and Geo-Engineering, University of Minnesota, Twin Cities, Minneapolis, MN 55455, USA}

\begin{abstract}
We present the first large-scale characterization of e-scooter speed behavior using 2.78 million GPS trajectories with per-point speed profiles from Swing, a major Korean operator, covering 52 cities and 382,328 users (May 2023), supplemented by 44.1 million trips over 24 months for longitudinal analysis. We find that 29.6\% of trips exceed the 25~km/h regulatory limit, but speeding is concentrated: 60.9\% of users never speed while 17.3\% speed on over half their trips. Spatial statistics (Getis-Ord $G_i^*$, Moran's $I$) identify significant speeding hotspots across five cities ($I = 0.16$--$0.71$, all $p < 0.001$). We estimate logistic regression with GEE, mixed-effects models, a two-part hurdle model, and a gradient-boosted tree with SHAP interpretability (AUC = 0.905) to disentangle rider demographics, operating mode, temporal context, and road infrastructure effects. The turbo (TUB) mode---which removes the software speed governor---dominates all models, increasing speeding odds 121-fold ($p < 0.001$) and SHAP importance (3.6$\times$ the next feature). A within-subject natural experiment ($n = 12{,}046$) confirms the governor reduces mean speed by 24\%, maximum speed by 31\%, and speeding from 7.4\% to 0.38\% (Cohen's $d = 1.0$--$2.2$). Longitudinal GEE analysis reveals monotonically increasing speeding with experience (3.8\% first trip to 28.0\% after 500+ trips; OR = 1.18 per doubling), driven by progressive TUB mode adoption rather than within-mode changes. A curvature analysis reveals a risk paradox: curvy roads have lower speeding prevalence but 5$\times$ higher risk scores when speeding occurs. Our findings support mandatory speed governors, targeted geofencing, and graduated mode access for new riders.
\end{abstract}
