\begin{abstract}
Speed is the primary modifiable risk factor for e-scooter injuries, yet no large-scale evidence exists on whether software speed governors---the most widely deployed technological countermeasure---actually reduce speeding. We address this gap using 21 million GPS-instrumented e-scooter trips from 52 South Korean cities (February--November 2023), exploiting variation across three speed modes offered by a single operator: ungoverned (TUB), standard (STD), and strict (ECO). Three independent lines of evidence converge. First, cross-sectional analysis reveals that the ungoverned TUB mode increases speeding odds 121-fold relative to the governed STD mode (GEE, $p < 0.001$), dominating all other predictors including age, time of day, and road type (SHAP importance 3.6$\times$ the next feature). Second, a natural experiment---the operator's system-wide removal of TUB mode in December 2023---provides causal evidence via difference-in-differences: cities with higher pre-ban TUB adoption experienced proportionally larger speeding reductions ($\beta = -0.568$, $p < 0.001$; dose-response $r = 0.754$), with no behavioral compensation on governed modes ($p = 0.59$). Third, survival analysis shows governors delay speeding onset 5.4-fold (median 81 vs.\ 15 trips to first speeding event; Cox time-varying HR $= 10.41$). These converging findings support mandatory speed governors, graduated mode access for new riders, and context-sensitive geofencing as evidence-based policy interventions.
\end{abstract}
