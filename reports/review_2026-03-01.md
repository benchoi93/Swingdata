## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. Staggered Difference-in-Differences for Speed Governor Causal Effects (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method:** The TUB, STD, and ECO modes were rolled out at different times across cities over the 24-month observation period (2022-2023). This creates a staggered adoption natural experiment. Apply the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) staggered DiD estimator with city-month panels. The treatment is "first month ECO mode becomes available in city c." The outcome is city-level mean speeding rate or mean max speed. Use never-treated cities (those without ECO) as controls.
- **Data needed:** `data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips with timestamps and modes). Aggregate to city-month panels. Mode availability per city-month can be inferred from first appearance of each mode in the data. All data already available.
- **Expected finding:** ECO mode introduction causally reduces city-level speeding by 5-15 percentage points, with heterogeneous effects across city size and baseline speeding levels. The effect should be larger in cities with higher pre-treatment TUB adoption.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Research Part C or Transportation Science (causal identification of speed governor effects has not been done; current evidence is correlational).
- **Connection to current work:** The current paper documents the TUB OR of 83-126 and the 20-fold speeding reduction from ECO mode, but these are associational. A causal DiD estimate would substantially strengthen the policy argument for mandatory speed governors. No published study has applied DiD to micromobility speed governor rollouts, representing a clear gap in the literature.
- **Key references:** [Callaway and Sant'Anna DiD methodology](https://matheusfacure.github.io/python-causality-handbook/13-Difference-in-Differences.html); [Brant Callaway 2025 DiD slides](https://cals.ncsu.edu/agricultural-and-resource-economics/wp-content/uploads/sites/46/2025/08/Difference-in-Differences_Slides_Callaway_Brant_CR_2025-compressed.pdf)

### 2. LSTM-Based Risky Trip Classification from Raw Speed Sequences (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method:** Train an LSTM or 1D-CNN on the raw within-trip speed vectors (variable-length time series of 5-50 readings per trip) to classify trips as safe/risky. Define "risky" as trips with max_speed > 25 km/h AND at least one harsh acceleration event. Use the existing LightGBM AUC=0.905 as a baseline. The key advantage of sequence models is that they can learn temporal patterns (e.g., "acceleration followed by sustained high speed on a straight segment") that tabular features cannot capture. Apply attention mechanisms to identify which time steps within a trip are most predictive of speeding.
- **Data needed:** Raw `speeds` column from `routes_all.parquet` (speed arrays available for all trips from 2023-02 onward). `trip_indicators.parquet` for labels. No new data collection needed.
- **Expected finding:** Sequence models should achieve AUC > 0.92 (exceeding the LightGBM baseline of 0.905) by capturing temporal dynamics. Attention weights should highlight the first 30 seconds and mid-trip acceleration phases as most predictive. This builds on the 2025 LSTM tandem riding detection paper by [Yurdakul et al. (2025)](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15389588.2025.2524466), which optimized 240-second input sequences for e-scooter trajectory classification.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Research Part C (methodological contribution: first application of sequence deep learning to within-trip speed safety classification for micromobility) or NeurIPS Workshop on ML for Transportation.
- **Connection to current work:** The current paper uses tabular features (mean speed, max speed, speed CV, etc.) extracted from speed profiles. Moving to raw sequence classification would exploit the full temporal structure of the 2.83M speed profiles, which is the dataset's unique strength. The SHAP analysis (Section 5.6) already shows that speed variability and acceleration features are among the top predictors; sequence models can capture these dynamics natively.

### 3. Hidden Markov Model for Rider Safety State Transitions Over Experience (Feasibility: MEDIUM)

- **Method:** Model each rider's longitudinal trip history (up to 500+ trips over 24 months) as a Hidden Markov Model with 3-4 latent states (e.g., "cautious newcomer," "experimenting," "habitual speeder," "reformed rider"). The observed emission is the per-trip speeding indicator (binary) and mean speed. Estimate transition probabilities as a function of trip rank and mode adoption. This extends the current GMM typology (which is cross-sectional) to a dynamic framework that captures how riders transition between behavioral states.
- **Data needed:** `data_parquet/modeling/trip_experience.parquet` (trip rank per user) + `data_parquet/trip_indicators.parquet` (per-trip speed metrics). Users with 50+ trips (approximately 50,000 users based on the experience analysis). All data already available.
- **Expected finding:** Most riders start in a "cautious" state and transition to "experimenting" around trips 10-20 (consistent with the current finding that TUB adoption rises from 11.5% to 47.8% over 50 trips). A subset (10-15%) transitions to "habitual speeder" and remains there, while another subset (5-10%) transitions back to "cautious" after an initial speeding phase ("reformed riders"). The transition probability to "speeder" should be mediated by TUB mode adoption.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Science (methodological: dynamic latent state model for micromobility behavior) or TR Part C.
- **Connection to current work:** The current paper's experience analysis (Section 5.5) shows monotonic speeding increase with experience (3.8% to 28.0%) and the GEE OR of 1.178 per log-trip. However, this assumes a homogeneous population trajectory. An HMM would reveal heterogeneous pathways -- some riders accelerate toward speeding faster than others, and some reverse course. This is directly relevant to the policy recommendation for graduated mode access, which implicitly assumes riders can be classified into dynamic safety states. The PI's expertise in TrajGAIL and generative trajectory models provides strong methodological alignment.
