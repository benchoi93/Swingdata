## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. **LSTM/Transformer Classification of Raw Speed Sequences for Real-Time Speeding Prediction** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method**: Train a sequence classifier (1D-CNN, LSTM, or lightweight Transformer) on raw per-trip speed vectors (~10s intervals, variable length) to predict the binary speeding outcome (is_speeding). The existing LightGBM model (AUC=0.905) uses hand-crafted features from these sequences; a sequence model could exploit the full temporal structure. Crucially, test the model in an _early-stopping_ setting: given only the first k readings of a trip (k = 3, 5, 10, corresponding to 30s, 50s, 100s), can we predict whether the rider will eventually exceed 25 km/h? This enables real-time intervention via the scooter's firmware. Analyze attention weights or saliency maps to identify which temporal features drive predictions. A recent LSTM-based e-scooter trajectory classifier for single vs. tandem riding detection ([Park et al., 2025, Traffic Injury Prevention](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15389588.2025.2524466)) demonstrates the viability of this approach for e-scooter behavioral classification.
- **Data needed**: `speeds` column from `routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips, already available). The `is_speeding` label is precomputed. No new data collection required. A 500K random subsample would suffice for training/validation.
- **Expected finding**: The sequence model should match or exceed AUC=0.905, with the early-stopping variant achieving AUC > 0.85 within the first 50 seconds of a trip. Today's mode-switcher finding implies that user identity (switcher vs. never-TUB) predicts riding style; early speed readings likely encode this identity implicitly. The practical value is a real-time speeding warning system that could preemptively throttle speed before violations occur.
- **Target venue**: NeurIPS Workshop on ML for Transportation / TR Part C / IEEE T-ITS
- **Connection to current work**: Today's SHAP analysis identified TUB mode as the dominant predictor (SHAP=2.33), but the sequence approach could discover temporal patterns (e.g., rapid acceleration in the first 30s predicts eventual speeding) that tabular features cannot capture. The PI's expertise in TrajGAIL and generative trajectory models makes this a natural methodological extension.

### 2. **Spatial Durbin Difference-in-Differences: Neighborhood Spillover Effects of the TUB Ban** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)

- **Method**: Extend the city-level TWFE DiD (Model 9) to a hex-level Spatial Durbin DiD. Build a monthly panel at H3 resolution 8 (~6,111 hexes across 50 cities) with hex-level speeding rate as the outcome and hex-level pre-ban TUB share as treatment intensity. Define spatial weights (queen contiguity or k-nearest-neighbor on hex centroids) and estimate both direct and indirect (spillover) effects of the TUB ban. This tests whether removing high-speed riders from one hex also reduces speeding in adjacent hexes through behavioral norm diffusion. Separately, use within-city variation in cycling infrastructure coverage (`frac_cycling_infra`) to estimate infrastructure effects via the spatial panel. Cicchino et al. (2024) found that speed-limiter settings affect sidewalk vs. road use ([Cicchino et al., 2024, TRR](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/03611981231214518)), suggesting spatial displacement effects that a hex-level model could quantify.
- **Data needed**: `routes_all.parquet` (aggregate to hex-month panels), `data_parquet/spatial/*.parquet` (H3 hex aggregates, already computed for May 2023, needs extension to all months), `data_parquet/osm_networks/*.gpkg` (road network). Computing monthly hex panels from 44M trips is the main data engineering task (~2-3 hours of compute).
- **Expected finding**: Positive spillover effects: when TUB disappears from hex i, adjacent hex j also experiences speeding reduction beyond its own TUB share change. The existing Moran's I values (0.21-0.71) indicate strong spatial autocorrelation, making spillover detection likely. Cycling infrastructure should show a protective direct effect (consistent with cold spots having 2x cycling infra coverage) and potentially a positive spillover (infrastructure in one hex benefits adjacent hexes).
- **Target venue**: Transportation Science / Journal of Regional Science / TR Part C
- **Connection to current work**: The current DiD operates at the city-month level (53 cities), which averages over substantial within-city spatial heterogeneity. Today's behavioral substitution finding (no compensation) was established at city and user level; the spatial DiD would extend this to neighborhoods, testing whether the ban's benefits diffuse spatially rather than just through individual behavioral channels.

### 3. **Survival Analysis for Time-to-First-Speeding: Experience Curves and Mode Adoption Dynamics** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method**: For the 1.82M users in the longitudinal dataset, model the time (measured in trip count) from a user's first trip to their first speeding event (max_speed > 25 km/h) using Cox proportional hazards or accelerated failure time models. Treat users who never speed as right-censored. Key covariates: mode adoption trajectory (when did they first use TUB?), city, age group, time-of-day patterns, and road composition of early trips. Test whether TUB mode adoption _mediates_ the experience-speeding relationship: does the hazard of first speeding increase when a rider first selects TUB mode, even after controlling for trip count? This separates the "learning" channel (riders get more comfortable over time) from the "mode selection" channel (riders discover TUB and instantly speed more). The already-established GEE finding (log(trip_rank) OR=1.178, p<10^-66) and the mode adoption curve (11.5% TUB at trip 1 to 47.8% at trip 50) provide strong prior evidence that mode adoption mediates the experience effect.
- **Data needed**: `routes_all.parquet` (user_id, trip_rank, mode, is_speeding, month_year). All data available. Filter to users with 5+ trips for reliable survival estimation (~500K users).
- **Expected finding**: The hazard of first speeding should spike when a user first adopts TUB mode (hazard ratio > 5). Controlling for TUB adoption, the residual effect of experience (trip count) on first speeding should be substantially attenuated, confirming that the experience effect is largely a mode-adoption artifact. Post-ban (December 2023), the survival time to first speeding should increase dramatically as the TUB pathway is removed. This provides a causal-mechanistic complement to today's DiD finding.
- **Target venue**: TR Part C / Transportation Science / Accident Analysis & Prevention
- **Connection to current work**: Today's mode-switcher analysis showed that switchers were inherently faster riders even in STD/ECO mode (pre-ban gap = 0.65 km/h). Survival analysis would reveal _when_ in their riding career this speed differential emerged and whether it predates or follows TUB adoption. If switchers were already faster before ever using TUB, it suggests self-selection rather than TUB-induced habituation -- a nuance the current cross-sectional comparison cannot resolve.
