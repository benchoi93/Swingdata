## Key Findings / Observations

### Behavioral Substitution DiD Results (n=563, 53 cities, 11 months)

| Outcome | Beta (per unit) | Effect/10pp | p-value | p-Bonf | Finding |
|---------|-----------------|-------------|---------|--------|---------|
| Overall speeding rate | -0.609 | -6.1pp | <0.001 | <0.001 | Validates Model 9 |
| Mean trip distance (km) | -0.038 | -0.004 | 0.35 | 1.00 | **No substitution** |
| Mean speed (STD/ECO) | +1.659 | +0.17 km/h | <0.001 | 0.001 | Composition effect |
| Mean max speed (STD/ECO) | +1.637 | +0.16 km/h | <0.001 | 0.001 | Composition effect |
| Speeding rate (STD/ECO) | -0.003 | -0.03pp | 0.47 | 1.00 | **No substitution** |

**Interpretation**: The TUB ban reduced overall speeding by 97%. Two substitution outcomes are significant after Bonferroni correction: mean and max speed within STD/ECO trips show a relative increase in high-treatment cities. However, this is a **composition effect** (former TUB riders joining the STD pool bring faster but still legal riding habits), not true risk compensation. Critically, the **STD/ECO speeding rate** shows no change (p=0.47) -- the speed governor ceiling is still effective. Trip distance also shows no change (p=0.35).

**Policy conclusion**: Speed governors reduce speeding without inducing offsetting risky behavior. The within-mode speed increase does not translate to speed limit violations.
