## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. **Behavioral Substitution After TUB Ban: Multi-Outcome DiD** (Feasibility: HIGH)
   - **Method:** Extend the existing TWFE DiD framework (Model 9, Equation 7 in the paper) to test for risk compensation effects following the December 2023 TUB mode ban. Use the same city-month panel (53 cities, 11 months, 563 observations) but replace the speeding rate dependent variable with alternative behavioral outcomes: (a) mean trip distance, (b) harsh acceleration rate (events per km), (c) speed coefficient of variation within STD-mode trips, (d) fraction of trip distance on major roads. Each outcome gets its own DiD regression. A joint null hypothesis test (Bonferroni-corrected for multiple outcomes) would provide a formal assessment of whether removing TUB mode induced any measurable behavioral offset.
   - **Data needed:** data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet (already available). Requires computing city-month averages of speed indicators for STD/ECO mode trips only. Trip indicators (trip_indicators.parquet) would need to be computed for non-May months, which is straightforward using existing src/compute_indicators.py.
   - **Expected finding:** Based on the current paper's finding that within-mode speeding rates are essentially unchanged between newcomers and established riders (experience effect is entirely mediated by TUB adoption, not within-mode behavior change), we hypothesize no significant risk compensation. A null result would be a strong policy finding: mandatory speed governors reduce speeding without inducing offsetting risky behavior.
   - **Target venue:** Accident Analysis and Prevention (safety policy focus) or Transportation Research Part C (extension of current paper).
   - **Connection to current work:** The conclusions already flag behavioral substitution as a future direction. This uses the same identification strategy, same panel data, and same econometric framework. It could be included as a robustness section in the current paper or serve as a short companion paper. A recent Nature Energy study on micromobility and car displacement ([Liao and Correia, 2022](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41560-022-01135-1)) used a similar natural experiment + geofencing approach.

### 2. **Early-Warning Speed Sequence Classification via 1D-CNN/Transformer** (Feasibility: HIGH)
   - **Method:** Treat each trip's raw speed vector (variable-length time series at ~10s intervals) as input to a 1D convolutional neural network or lightweight Transformer encoder. The primary task is binary classification: will this trip exceed 25 km/h at any point? The novel contribution is an *early-warning* variant: predict eventual speeding using only the first K speed readings (K = 3, 6, 9, corresponding to 30s, 60s, 90s into the trip). This creates a practical real-time intervention tool. Training on 80% of May 2023 trips (2.2M), testing on 20% (0.56M). Baselines: (a) existing LightGBM with static features (AUC=0.905), (b) logistic regression on first-K summary statistics.
   - **Data needed:** Raw speed arrays from the speeds column in data_parquet/routes_filtered.parquet (2.78M trips, already available). Mode and road class features from trip_indicators.parquet and trip_road_classes.parquet for contextual conditioning.
   - **Expected finding:** Full-trip sequence classification should match or modestly exceed the LightGBM baseline (AUC > 0.90). The early-warning model is the key novelty: we hypothesize AUC > 0.80 at 30s and > 0.85 at 60s, because the current paper's speed drift finding (-1.5 km/h in second half) and the high SHAP importance of acceleration-related features suggest that early speed dynamics are strongly predictive. This has immediate practical value for dynamic geofencing. A 2025 study by [Yurdakul (2025)](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1155/atr/8794166) compared 19 ML/DL models for e-scooter usage prediction, but no study has applied sequence models to within-trip speed safety classification at this scale.
   - **Target venue:** NeurIPS Workshop on Machine Learning for Transportation, or Transportation Research Part C (framed as a real-time safety monitoring system).
   - **Connection to current work:** Builds directly on two key findings: (a) the SHAP analysis showing TUB mode dominance (2.33) with acceleration and cruise fraction as top non-mode features, and (b) the speed drift finding. Aligns with the PI's expertise in deep generative models for transportation (TrajGAIL).

### 3. **Hidden Markov Model for Rider Safety State Transitions Over 24 Months** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)
   - **Method:** Model each user's 24-month trip history as a sequence of latent safety states using a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) with 3-4 hidden states (e.g., safe, moderate risk, high risk, disengaged). Observable emissions are per-trip features: speeding binary, mean speed, speed CV, mode used (TUB/STD/ECO), trip distance. The transition matrix reveals how riders move between safety states over time. Key research questions: (a) What fraction of riders transition from safe to high risk vs. remaining stable? (b) Does the December 2023 TUB ban cause a measurable shift in transition probabilities (regime change in the HMM)? (c) Are there identifiable warning sequences? Estimate using the Baum-Welch algorithm on the 1.82M user panel. For computational feasibility, subsample to users with 10+ trips.
   - **Data needed:** data_parquet/all_months/user_longitudinal.parquet (1.82M users, already available) and data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet (44.1M trips). The GMM rider typology (K=4) from the current paper provides a natural initialization for HMM emission distributions.
   - **Expected finding:** We expect 3-4 stable states corresponding roughly to the GMM clusters. The novel finding would be the transition dynamics: the current paper shows that experience increases speeding (3.8% to 28.0%), mediated by TUB adoption (11.5% to 47.8%). The HMM would formalize this as a transition probability from safe to moderate risk that increases with experience. The December 2023 TUB ban should appear as a structural break. A longitudinal study from [Riyadh (2024)](https://www.mdpi.com/2071-1050/16/9/3759) found temporal shifts in rider perspectives, but no study has modeled individual rider safety trajectories at this scale using latent state models.
   - **Target venue:** Transportation Science (methodological contribution) or Transportation Research Part C (applied safety).
   - **Connection to current work:** Directly extends two analyses: (a) the GMM rider typology (Model 4), which provides static cluster assignments but ignores temporal dynamics, and (b) the GEE experience analysis (Model 6), which shows a monotonic aggregate trend but cannot capture individual-level transitions. The 24-month user panel (1.82M users) is a unique asset that no comparable e-scooter study possesses. This direction would transform the static rider typology into a dynamic behavioral model.
