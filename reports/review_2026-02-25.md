## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. **Causal Impact of Speed Governor Policy via Staggered Difference-in-Differences** (Feasibility: HIGH)
   - **Method**: The 24-month dataset covers multiple cities where TUB mode rollout timing likely varied. Use a staggered difference-in-differences design (Callaway & Sant'Anna 2021) with city-month panels to estimate the causal effect of TUB mode availability on aggregate speeding rates. The existing data already contains the mode field by month by city -- no new data collection is needed. Alternatively, exploit the ECO mode introduction date (if identifiable) as a policy intervention.
   - **Data needed**: `data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips with month, city, mode). Aggregate to city-month panel. Already available.
   - **Expected finding**: TUB mode introduction causally increased city-level speeding by 15-25 percentage points, with heterogeneous effects across city size and infrastructure density. This would strengthen the current paper's correlational TUB finding with a causal identification strategy.
   - **Target venue**: Transportation Research Part C or Transportation Science (causal inference in transportation is highly valued)
   - **Connection to current work**: The current paper establishes TUB mode OR = 83-126 correlatively. A DiD design would provide causal identification, addressing the self-selection concern (riders who choose TUB may be inherently riskier). A recent geofencing study by Yang & Kang (2023, J-ITS) established the methodology for e-scooter policy evaluation but did not examine speed governor effects causally. This gap is directly addressable with the Swing dataset.

### 2. **Deep Sequence Classification of Speed Profiles for Real-Time Risk Scoring** (Feasibility: HIGH)
   - **Method**: Train a 1D-CNN or Transformer encoder on the raw per-trip speed vectors (variable-length time series from the `speeds` column) to classify trips as safe vs. risky in real-time. Use the first N seconds of a trip to predict whether the full trip will involve speeding. Benchmark against the LightGBM SHAP model (AUC = 0.905) which uses handcrafted features. The deep learning model may capture speed dynamics (acceleration patterns, speed ramp-up signatures) that the feature-engineered model misses.
   - **Data needed**: Raw `speeds` arrays from `data_parquet/cleaned/routes_cleaned.parquet` (2.78M trips). Already available. Variable-length sequences require padding or a set-based architecture.
   - **Expected finding**: A Transformer model on raw speed sequences achieves AUC > 0.92 (exceeding the 0.905 from feature-engineered LightGBM), and the first 30 seconds of a trip are sufficient to predict full-trip speeding with AUC > 0.85, enabling real-time intervention.
   - **Target venue**: NeurIPS/ICML Workshop on AI for Transportation, or IEEE T-ITS
   - **Connection to current work**: The current paper uses handcrafted speed indicators (mean, max, CV, cruise fraction). A deep learning approach on the raw speed sequences would complement this by capturing non-linear temporal patterns. This aligns directly with Dr. Choi's expertise in deep generative models and spatiotemporal data (TrajGAIL, probabilistic forecasting). A recent Yurdakul (2025) study in J. Advanced Transportation applied ML/DL to e-scooter usage prediction (R2 = 0.92) but did not tackle safety classification from speed profiles -- a clear gap.

### 3. **Hidden Markov Model for Latent Rider Safety State Transitions Over 24 Months** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)
   - **Method**: Model each rider's longitudinal trip history as a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) with 3-4 latent states (e.g., "cautious", "exploratory", "habitual speeder", "reformed"). The observed emissions are trip-level speed indicators; the hidden states capture unobserved rider safety disposition. Estimate transition probabilities to understand how riders move between safety states over their lifetime. Use the 24-month panel of 1.82M users. Focus on the ~100K users with 101+ trips for reliable state estimation.
   - **Data needed**: `data_parquet/modeling/trip_experience.parquet` (trip-level experience features), merged with trip indicators. Already available. Need to construct per-user time series of speeding indicators.
   - **Expected finding**: Most riders (60%+) remain in a "cautious" absorbing state. Among riders who transition to riskier states, the median time-to-transition is 20-30 trips. TUB mode adoption is the strongest predictor of state transitions. A small fraction (5-10%) show "reformation" (transition from habitual speeder back to cautious), potentially after a near-miss or seasonal break. This would extend the current paper's static GMM typology into a dynamic model of behavior change.
   - **Target venue**: Transportation Research Part C or Transportation Science
   - **Connection to current work**: The current paper's GMM identifies 4 static rider types. The HMM would make this dynamic, modeling how riders transition between types over time. The experience analysis (3.8% -> 28.0% speeding) is currently descriptive; an HMM would provide a generative model of the underlying process. A 2025 study by Neshagaran, Cherry & Sanders (TRR) studied observational rider behavior but lacked longitudinal tracking. The Swing dataset's 24-month depth uniquely enables this analysis. This also connects to Dr. Choi's work on trajectory prediction and latent state modeling.
