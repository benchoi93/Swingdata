## Issues Found

### 1. [MAJOR] Summary coefficient figure (fig_did_summary) conflates incomparable scales

The summary coefficient plot places four estimates on the same x-axis ("Effect on speeding rate (pp)"), but they are not comparable:

- **Cross-sectional OLS** (-4.9pp) and **Dose-Response** (+5.0pp): These are marginal effects *per 10pp change* in TUB share.
- **TWFE DiD** (-56.8pp) and **Event Study** (-59.2pp): These are the *full interaction coefficient multiplied by 100*, representing the effect for a city with 100% pre-ban TUB share.

Showing these on the same axis is misleading. A city with 50% pre-ban TUB share would see a 28.4pp reduction from TWFE, which is consistent with 5.0pp per 10pp from dose-response. The figure should either normalize all estimates to the same scale (e.g., "per 10pp TUB share") or clearly annotate the different denominators.

- **Suggested fix**: Rescale TWFE and event study to "per 10pp" (divide by 10), or show the "at 50% pre-TUB share" interpretation for all four models. Alternatively, add a footnote to the figure explaining the different scales.

### 2. [MAJOR] Event study pre-trends concern is understated

The report acknowledges that Feb-Mar coefficients are significant and attributes this to "platform ramp-up." However, examining the actual coefficients:

| Period | Coefficient | p-value |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| k=-6 (May) | +0.110 | 0.031 |
| k=-5 (Jun) | +0.107 | 0.006 |
| k=-4 (Jul) | +0.055 | 0.012 |
| k=-3 (Aug) | +0.071 | 0.0004 |
| k=-2 (Sep) | +0.064 | 0.0007 |

Even restricting to Apr-Oct, 5 of 6 pre-period coefficients are significantly different from zero (Oct is the reference). The Apr-Oct coefficients show a declining trend from +0.11 to +0.06, suggesting that cities with higher TUB adoption saw *gradually declining* speeding rates relative to the reference period. This means the Dec 2023 coefficient may partially reflect a continuation of this pre-existing trend, not just the treatment effect.

- **Suggested fix**: (a) Report a joint F-test for all pre-period coefficients being zero, not just individual significance. (b) Consider a "de-trended" event study that removes the linear pre-trend. (c) The planned restricted window robustness check (Task 11.3.1) is critical and should be prioritized. (d) Consider using Nov as the reference month instead of Oct, since Nov is the last pre-treatment month.

### 3. [MINOR] Panel dimension reported inconsistently

The report and TASKS.md state "53 cities x 11 months = 563 obs," but 53 x 11 = 583. The actual 563 reflects that three cities (Jeju: 5 months, Mokpo: 5 months, Ulsan: 3 months) have incomplete monthly coverage. This should be reported explicitly to avoid confusion.

- **Suggested fix**: State "53 cities across 11 months (563 obs; 3 cities with incomplete monthly coverage)" in the report.

### 4. [MINOR] Two different correlation coefficients for the same relationship

The dec_transition figure (from feasibility script) reports r=0.776 (n=50 cities, filtered to >1000 Nov trips), while the dose-response figure (from analysis script) reports r=0.754 (n=51 cities, unfiltered). Both are correct for their respective samples, but the report text cites only r=0.754. The discrepancy could confuse readers comparing figures.

- **Suggested fix**: Apply consistent sample filtering across both figures, or clearly label the sample restriction in each figure caption.

### 5. [MINOR] Code duplication: summary figure generated twice

In `src/did_analysis.py`, the `generate_figures()` function creates a placeholder `fig_did_summary.pdf` (lines 489-509) that is immediately overwritten by `main()` (lines 530-571). The placeholder code should be removed.

- **Suggested fix**: Remove the summary figure code from `generate_figures()` and keep only the version in `main()` that uses actual model results.

### 6. [MINOR] fig_did_summary label overlap

In the summary coefficient plot, the "-4.9pp" text label for the cross-sectional OLS estimate overlaps with the bar, making it hard to read. The label positioning logic (`coef - ci - 0.5 if coef < 0 else coef + ci + 0.5`) does not handle cases where the bar is very small relative to the axis range.

- **Suggested fix**: Use a fixed offset or adaptive positioning based on the axis range, not the individual bar width.

### 7. [MINOR] No standard errors or confidence intervals for dose-response r

The dose-response correlation r=0.754 is reported with a p-value but without a confidence interval. For publication, bootstrapped 95% CIs for the correlation (or the slope) would strengthen the reporting.

- **Suggested fix**: Add bootstrapped 95% CI for the dose-response slope or use Fisher z-transformation for the correlation CI.

## Recommendations

- **Prioritize the restricted-window robustness check (Task 11.3.1)** before any attempt to integrate DiD results into the paper. The pre-trends concern is the single biggest threat to the causal interpretation.
- **Consider a synthetic control method (SCM)** as a complement to DiD for the largest cities (Seoul, Busan, Daejeon). SCM does not require the parallel trends assumption and provides transparent counterfactual construction. With 53 donor cities and 10 pre-treatment months, there is sufficient data.
- **Report the "28pp at 50% pre-TUB" interpretation consistently** across all four models rather than the raw coefficients, which have different scales and are hard to compare directly.
- **Add a Bacon decomposition** (Goodman-Bacon 2021) to decompose the TWFE estimate into its constituent 2x2 DiD comparisons. Since there is only one post-treatment period, the decomposition is straightforward, but it would demonstrate methodological rigor.
- **Consider clustering standard errors at the city level** in the TWFE model. Currently HC1 is used, which corrects for heteroscedasticity but not within-city serial correlation. With 11 time periods per city, cluster-robust SEs (Arellano 1987) may be more appropriate. The `cov_type='cluster'` option in statsmodels with `cov_kwds={'groups': panel['city']}` would address this.
- **The city_trends figure (fig_did_city_trends) is very informative** -- it shows that TUB-mode speeding drops to near-zero in Dec 2023 while STD/ECO remain flat. Consider promoting this to a main paper figure rather than supplementary material, as it provides the clearest visual evidence of the causal mechanism.
- **For the parallel_trends figure**, the dual-axis design (speeding rate + TUB share) effectively shows the co-movement. However, the x-axis labels are truncated to just month numbers ("02", "03"...), which may be unclear. Add "2023-" prefix or use abbreviated month names.

## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. **Synthetic Control Method for City-Level Causal Estimates** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method**: Apply Abadie et al. (2010) synthetic control to construct counterfactual speeding trajectories for individual cities. For each treated city (those with >50% pre-ban TUB share), construct a synthetic control from a weighted combination of low-TUB cities. The gap between actual and synthetic post-treatment provides a city-specific causal estimate. Use permutation inference (placebo tests on control cities) for statistical validity.
- **Data needed**: The existing `city_month_panel.parquet` (563 obs) is sufficient. No additional data required. Cities with very low pre-ban TUB share (e.g., Gimpo at 0.3% drop) serve as the donor pool.
- **Expected finding**: City-specific treatment effects will correlate strongly with pre-ban TUB share (confirming dose-response), but SCM avoids the parallel trends assumption that threatens the DiD results. Cities with unusual pre-trends (Seoul, Ulsan) will have informative synthetic controls.
- **Target venue**: Transportation Research Part C (as a methodological extension to the current paper) or Journal of Causal Inference (standalone methods paper).
- **Connection to current work**: Directly addresses the pre-trends violation identified in Issue 2. SCM results serve as a robustness check complementing the four DiD models. Recent work by [Assessing safety effectiveness of speed limit reduction (TR Part A, 2022)](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0965856422000040) used spatial DiD for speed limit policies; applying SCM to e-scooter speed governors would be novel.

### 2. **Within-Trip Speed Sequence Classification via Transformers** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method**: Train a 1D Transformer or temporal convolutional network (TCN) on raw per-trip speed sequences (variable-length, ~10s intervals) to classify trips as safe vs. risky. Use the December 2023 natural experiment as a validation opportunity: the model should predict near-zero risk for post-ban trips if it truly captures TUB-mode speed dynamics rather than just mode labels. Implement attention visualization to identify which portions of the speed profile (acceleration phase, cruise phase, deceleration) contribute most to risk classification. Compare against the LightGBM baseline (AUC=0.905) already established in Phase 8.4.
- **Data needed**: Raw `speeds` arrays from `routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips). Already available. Subsample 500K trips stratified by mode and pre/post ban. Labels: binary speeding indicator or continuous speeding rate.
- **Expected finding**: The Transformer will achieve AUC > 0.92 (exceeding LightGBM 0.905) and attention maps will highlight speed peaks in the cruise phase as the dominant predictor, consistent with the SHAP finding that TUB mode is the top feature. Post-ban trips will cluster tightly in the "safe" class, providing an external validation of the model's causal understanding.
- **Target venue**: NeurIPS Workshop on Machine Learning for Autonomous Driving, or Transportation Research Part C (methods + application).
- **Connection to current work**: Extends the SHAP analysis (Phase 8.4) from feature importance to temporal dynamics. The PI's expertise in deep generative models (TrajGAIL) and sequence modeling provides a natural methodological bridge. The December 2023 ban provides a unique external validation opportunity rarely available in transportation ML studies. Recent work on [e-scooter maneuver recognition (UbiComp 2023)](https://dl.acm.org/doi/10.1145/3570345) and [e-scooter trajectory dynamics (2024)](https://www.danulahettiachchi.com/papers/ahs24.pdf) has used sensor data for behavior classification, but none have exploited a natural experiment for model validation.

### 3. **Behavioral Substitution and Compensating Risk After TUB Ban** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)

- **Method**: Investigate whether riders compensated for the TUB ban by engaging in riskier behavior on other dimensions. Using the December 2023 natural experiment, compare pre-ban vs. post-ban trips on: (a) harsh acceleration frequency, (b) trip distance (did riders take longer trips to compensate for lower speeds?), (c) road class selection (did riders shift to faster roads?), (d) time-of-day patterns (did riders shift to off-peak hours?). Use a DiD framework with the same panel structure but different outcome variables. This tests the "risk compensation" hypothesis (Peltzman 1975) -- that safety interventions may be partially offset by behavioral adaptation.
- **Data needed**: Extend `city_month_panel.parquet` with additional outcome variables: mean harsh accel rate, mean trip distance, frac_major_road, night-riding share. Requires re-running the panel construction with additional aggregated columns from `routes_all.parquet`. Moderate additional computation.
- **Expected finding**: Partial risk compensation -- riders may shift to slightly longer trips or different road types after the ban, but the net safety effect remains strongly positive (the 28pp speeding reduction far exceeds any compensating behavior). If no compensation is found, this strengthens the policy recommendation.
- **Target venue**: Transportation Research Part C (as a section in the current paper, adding ~2 pages) or Accident Analysis & Prevention (standalone).
- **Connection to current work**: Directly extends the DiD framework (Phase 11) to multiple outcomes. The existing trip-level indicators (Phase 2) and road class data (Phase 3) provide all the outcome variables needed. The risk compensation hypothesis is a standard concern in speed policy evaluations -- see [Cicchino et al. (2024)](https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1177/03611981231214518) who found that lower speed limits led to more sidewalk riding, suggesting behavioral adaptation. Testing this with the Korean data would be a valuable complement to the speed governor analysis.
