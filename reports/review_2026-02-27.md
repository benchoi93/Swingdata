## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. Causal Estimation of Speed Governor Effects via Staggered Difference-in-Differences (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method:** The Swing dataset contains natural variation in mode availability across cities and time periods (ECO mode was introduced at different times in different cities during 2022-2023). Apply a staggered difference-in-differences (DiD) estimator -- specifically the Callaway and Sant'Anna (2021) estimator that handles heterogeneous treatment timing -- to estimate the causal effect of ECO mode introduction on speeding rates. The 24-month longitudinal data with city-level variation in mode rollout provides a textbook natural experiment setting.
- **Data needed:** `data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips with mode, city, month). Need to identify the first month each city had ECO mode available -- this can be derived from the existing data by finding the earliest ECO trip per city. Already available.
- **Expected finding:** ECO mode introduction causally reduces city-level speeding rates by 5-15 percentage points, with heterogeneous effects by city size and baseline speeding rate. The within-subject paired analysis (n=12,046) already shows the individual-level effect is massive (speeding drops from 54% to 1.1%), but the city-level causal estimate would be policy-relevant and methodologically stronger than the current cross-sectional comparisons.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Research Part C (policy evaluation), or Transportation Science (causal inference methods)
- **Connection to current work:** The current paper establishes the TUB/ECO effect descriptively and via within-subject pairing. A causal DiD paper would be a natural companion that elevates the evidence from association to causation, directly addressing reviewer concerns about selection bias (riders who choose ECO may be inherently safer).
- **Key reference gap:** Web search confirms no published DiD evaluation of e-scooter speed governor policies as of 2025. This would be a first.

### 2. Transformer-Based Speed Profile Classification for Real-Time Risk Scoring (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method:** Train a sequence classifier on the raw within-trip speed vectors (variable-length time series at ~10s intervals) to predict trip-level outcomes: (a) binary speeding occurrence, (b) GMM rider class membership, (c) sustained high-speed episode detection. Use a lightweight Transformer encoder (similar to the PI's jin2022transformer map-matching work) with positional encoding to handle variable-length sequences. Compare against 1D-CNN and LSTM baselines. The SHAP analysis (AUC=0.905) already shows that engineered features predict speeding well; the question is whether raw speed sequences contain additional predictive information beyond hand-crafted indicators.
- **Data needed:** Raw speed vectors from `speeds` column (already parsed in trip_indicators computation). The 2.78M May 2023 trips provide ample training data. Road class sequences from `trip_road_classes.parquet` can serve as auxiliary input. Already available.
- **Expected finding:** The Transformer model achieves AUC > 0.92 (exceeding LightGBM's 0.905 on engineered features) by capturing sequential speed dynamics that engineered indicators miss -- particularly speed oscillation patterns and context-dependent acceleration. Attention weights reveal that speed readings near the start and middle of trips are most predictive, consistent with the "ramp-up" and "cruise" phases identified in the current paper.
- **Target venue:** NeurIPS (if framed as time-series classification methodology), TR Part C (if framed as real-time safety tool), or IEEE T-ITS (intelligent transportation systems)
- **Connection to current work:** The current paper computes 15+ engineered speed indicators. This direction asks whether the raw speed signal contains more. It also connects to the PI's expertise in transformers for transportation (jin2022transformer) and deep generative models for trajectory data (TrajGAIL). A practical outcome would be a real-time risk scoring API that operators like Swing could deploy.
- **Key reference gap:** A 2025 systematic review of ML in micromobility (arXiv:2508.16135) confirms that deep learning on within-trip speed profiles for safety classification is an unexplored area -- existing ML work focuses on demand prediction and spatial patterns, not within-trip behavior.

### 3. Experience-Mediated Mode Adoption: A Structural Equation Model of the Speeding Escalation Pathway (Feasibility: MEDIUM)

- **Method:** The current paper establishes two key findings that are analyzed separately: (a) experience increases speeding (OR=1.178 per log-trip), and (b) TUB mode adoption increases with experience (11.5% at trip 1 to 47.8% at trip 50). The mediation analysis implied in the paper (experience drives mode choice, which drives speeding) has not been formally tested. Fit a structural equation model (SEM) or causal mediation analysis (Baron-Kenny + bootstrap) to decompose the total experience effect into: (i) direct effect of experience on speeding (holding mode constant), (ii) indirect effect mediated through TUB mode adoption, and (iii) indirect effect mediated through road familiarity (riders learn faster routes on major roads). Use the 24-month longitudinal data with trip-level observations.
- **Data needed:** `data_parquet/modeling/trip_experience.parquet` (trip rank, experience bins), `data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet` (mode per trip), `data_parquet/trip_road_classes.parquet` (road composition). May need to compute per-user route entropy (are experienced riders using more consistent routes?). Mostly available; route entropy is a new computation but straightforward.
- **Expected finding:** 60-80% of the experience-speeding association is mediated by TUB mode adoption (the indirect path), with the remaining 20-40% attributable to increased comfort/risk tolerance. The within-STD/ECO analysis in the current paper already hints at this (newcomer and established riders show similar speeding within the same mode), but a formal mediation model would quantify the decomposition.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Research Part C (behavioral modeling), Accident Analysis & Prevention (safety behavior)
- **Connection to current work:** Directly builds on the experience analysis (Task 8.1) and the mode adoption finding. Transforms a descriptive observation ("experience increases speeding because riders switch to TUB mode") into a formally tested causal mediation pathway. Policy implication: restricting TUB access for new riders (graduated mode access) would interrupt the dominant mediation pathway.
