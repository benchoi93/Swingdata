1. **Causal Effect of Speed Governors via Staggered Difference-in-Differences** (Feasibility: HIGH)
   - Method: Exploit the staggered rollout of TUB/ECO modes across Korean cities and months (2022-2023) as a natural experiment. Use Callaway-Sant'Anna (2021) staggered DiD estimator to estimate the causal effect of mode introduction on city-level speeding rates. The 24-month longitudinal dataset with 52 cities provides the necessary panel structure.
   - Data needed: `data_parquet/all_months/routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips with timestamps and modes), city-level monthly aggregates. Already available. Need to identify exact month of TUB/ECO rollout per city from the data.
   - Expected finding: Speed governor modes causally reduce speeding rates by 15-30 percentage points, with heterogeneous treatment effects by city size and baseline speeding level. The TUB OR=83-126 from cross-sectional models likely overstates the causal effect due to selection bias (risk-seekers choose TUB mode).
   - Target venue: Transportation Research Part C or Transportation Science
   - Connection to current work: The current paper documents massive TUB/ECO effects but cannot establish causality. A recent Nature Energy study ([Impacts of micromobility on car displacement with evidence from a natural experiment and geofencing policy](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41560-022-01135-1)) demonstrates the feasibility and publication potential of natural experiment designs in micromobility.

2. **Deep Sequence Classification of Speed Profiles for Real-Time Risk Prediction** (Feasibility: HIGH)
   - Method: Train a 1D-CNN or Transformer encoder on the raw per-trip speed sequences (variable-length time series) for binary classification (speeding vs. non-speeding trip). Critically, predict speeding using only the first 30-50% of the speed sequence, enabling real-time intervention. Use SHAP on the sequence model to identify which temporal patterns (early acceleration bursts, speed ramp-ups) are most predictive.
   - Data needed: `speeds` column from raw trip data (already parsed in indicator computation). Sample 500K trips stratified by mode and speeding outcome. Requires tokenizing variable-length speed sequences.
   - Expected finding: Early-trip speed dynamics (first 3-5 readings) predict eventual speeding with AUC > 0.85. Specific temporal signatures (monotonic acceleration in first 30s, speed exceeding 20 km/h within first 2 readings) provide >80% positive predictive value, enabling real-time speed governor activation.
   - Target venue: NeurIPS (ML application) or TR Part C (transportation focus)
   - Connection to current work: The current paper uses aggregate trip-level indicators. The raw speed sequences are the richest underexploited data asset. This builds on the PI's expertise in sequence models (TrajGAIL) and the current LightGBM+SHAP framework.

3. **Hidden Markov Model for Latent Rider Safety State Transitions** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)
   - Method: For users with 50+ trips (substantial subset of 1.82M users), model each user's trip-level speeding sequence as a Hidden Markov Model with 3-4 latent states (e.g., cautious, learning, habitual speeder, reformed). Estimate transition matrices to understand how riders move between safety states over time. Test whether mode switching (STD to TUB) drives state transitions or vice versa.
   - Data needed: `data_parquet/all_months/user_longitudinal.parquet` (1.82M users with trip histories), `data_parquet/modeling/trip_experience.parquet` (trip rank). Already available. Need to construct per-user speeding time series.
   - Expected finding: Most users follow a cautious-to-risk trajectory (consistent with the 3.8% to 28.0% experience learning curve), but a subset (~10-15%) transitions back to safer riding after an initial risky phase ("reformation" state). TUB mode adoption is the primary driver of state transitions, with the cautious-to-risk transition mediated almost entirely by mode choice rather than inherent behavior change.
   - Target venue: Transportation Science or TR Part C
   - Connection to current work: The GMM rider typology (4 classes) is static. An HMM would capture dynamic transitions between rider types over time, directly extending the "experience drives mode adoption, mode drives speeding" causal chain identified in Phase 8.
