## Recommendations

1. **Reconcile TWFE betas before paper integration.** If the behavioral substitution analysis is integrated into the paper, ensure the overall speeding outcome uses the same panel and treatment variable as Model 9 to avoid any confusion about why the betas differ (-0.609 vs -0.568). The simplest approach is to reuse `city_month_panel.parquet` and its `tub_share_pre` column as the treatment variable in the behavioral substitution script.

2. **Strengthen the composition vs. compensation distinction.** The report correctly identifies the STD/ECO speed increase as a "composition effect" (former TUB riders joining the STD pool). To make this argument airtight for reviewers, consider adding a user-level analysis: track individual riders who used TUB before December 2023 and STD/ECO after, comparing their post-ban STD speeds to the STD speeds of riders who never used TUB. If TUB-switchers ride faster in STD than never-TUB riders, it is composition; if they ride the same, it is compensation.

3. **Consider reporting effect sizes alongside p-values for the substitution analysis.** The Bonferroni-corrected p-values are informative, but reviewers may also want to see the practical significance. The STD/ECO mean speed effect of +0.17 km/h per 10pp TUB reduction is statistically significant but substantively small (a 50% pre-ban TUB city would see +0.85 km/h mean speed increase within STD/ECO, from ~13.7 to ~14.5 km/h -- still well below 25 km/h). Stating this explicitly strengthens the "no meaningful risk compensation" conclusion.

## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. **Within-Trip Speed Sequence Classification via Transformer/LSTM** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method**: Train a 1D-CNN, LSTM, or lightweight Transformer classifier on raw per-trip speed vectors (variable-length time series of ~10s readings) to predict binary speeding outcome. Use the existing 2.78M labeled trips (is_speeding as ground truth). Compare against the LightGBM baseline (AUC=0.905) and analyze attention weights to identify which portions of the trip are most predictive of speeding.
- **Data needed**: The `speeds` column from `routes_all.parquet` (already available as variable-length arrays). No new data collection required.
- **Expected finding**: The sequence model should match or exceed LightGBM AUC by capturing temporal dynamics (e.g., acceleration patterns preceding speeding events, speed buildup trajectories) that tabular features cannot represent. Attention maps would reveal that speed readings in the first 30-60 seconds are highly predictive -- enabling real-time intervention. This would also connect to recent work on LSTM-based classification of e-scooter trajectories for behavioral detection ([Park et al., 2025, Traffic Injury Prevention](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/15389588.2025.2524466)).
- **Target venue**: NeurIPS Workshop on ML for Transportation / TR Part C / IEEE T-ITS
- **Connection to current work**: Today's SHAP analysis (Model 8) treats speed profiles as hand-crafted tabular features. A sequence model would exploit the raw temporal structure, providing a richer representation. The PI's expertise in deep generative models (TrajGAIL) and transformers makes this a natural extension.

### 2. **User-Level Behavioral Trajectory Modeling with Hidden Markov Models** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method**: For the 1.82M users with longitudinal trip histories (24 months), define 3-4 latent behavioral states (e.g., "cautious", "moderate", "aggressive") based on per-trip speed indicators. Fit a Hidden Markov Model (or its Bayesian extension) to each user's sequence of trip-level states over time. Estimate transition probabilities to quantify: (a) how quickly riders transition from cautious to aggressive, (b) whether the TUB ban forced state transitions (aggressive -> moderate), and (c) whether state transitions are absorbing (once aggressive, always aggressive) or reversible.
- **Data needed**: `routes_all.parquet` (trip-level, time-ordered by user) and `trip_indicators.parquet`. Already available. Filter to users with 10+ trips for reliable estimation.
- **Expected finding**: Based on the GEE experience results (speeding increases monotonically with trip rank, mediated by TUB adoption), the HMM should reveal that transitions from cautious to aggressive states are driven by mode switching (TUB adoption). Post-ban (December 2023), we expect forced transitions back to moderate states, with persistence depending on individual riding style. The "stop-and-go" GMM cluster (8%) may emerge as a distinct latent state with unique transition dynamics. This would address a gap identified in a recent systematic review of e-scooter user behavior ([Torrisi et al., 2025, Transport Reviews](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/01441647.2025.2545221)).
- **Target venue**: Transportation Science / TR Part C
- **Connection to current work**: The GMM typology (Model 4) provides a cross-sectional snapshot of rider types. The HMM would add a dynamic dimension, modeling how riders move between types over time. Today's behavioral substitution finding (no risk compensation post-ban) could be further validated by showing that state transitions post-ban are toward safer states, not compensatory aggressive states.

### 3. **Spatial Spillover and Infrastructure Causal Effects via Spatial Durbin DiD** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)

- **Method**: Extend the current TWFE DiD (Model 9) to a Spatial Durbin Difference-in-Differences (SD-DiD) framework. The H3 hex-level data (6,111 cells across 50 cities) provides the spatial resolution. Define spatial weights (queen contiguity or k-NN on hex centroids) and test whether the TUB ban's effect on speeding in one hex spills over to adjacent hexes. Additionally, exploit within-city variation in cycling infrastructure coverage (`frac_cycling_infra` at hex level) as a "dose" variable to estimate the causal effect of infrastructure on speeding, using the hex-level panel.
- **Data needed**: `data_parquet/spatial/*.parquet` (H3 hex-level aggregates, already computed) and `data_parquet/osm_networks/*.gpkg` (road network attributes). Would need to compute monthly hex-level panels from `routes_all.parquet` for the temporal dimension. The spatial autocorrelation results (Moran's I = 0.21-0.71) already demonstrate strong spatial structure.
- **Expected finding**: Spillover effects are likely positive: when TUB mode disappears from one hex (reducing speeding), adjacent hexes also experience reductions beyond what their own TUB share change would predict -- a "norm diffusion" effect. The cycling infrastructure effect is expected to be protective (cold spots already show 2x cycling infra coverage vs. hot spots), but the current cross-sectional association may be confounded by unobserved hex characteristics. The spatial DiD would provide a stronger causal estimate. This connects to recent work using spatial analysis with e-scooter GPS data at the city scale ([Grosso et al., 2025, Scientific Reports](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-06938-2)).
- **Target venue**: Journal of Regional Science / TR Part C / Transportation Science
- **Connection to current work**: Today's DiD operates at the city-month level (53 cities). The spatial DiD would operate at the hex-month level (potentially thousands of hexes), exploiting within-city spatial variation that the city-level model cannot capture. The existing Moran's I and Gi* results (Phase 4) provide the theoretical motivation, and the OSM network data (Phase 3) provides the infrastructure covariates.
