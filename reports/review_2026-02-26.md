## Suggested Research Directions

### 1. **Causal Estimation of Speed Governor Effects via Staggered Adoption Design** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method:** The 24-month dataset covers periods when different cities adopted TUB/ECO modes at different times. This creates a staggered difference-in-differences design (Callaway & Sant'Anna 2021). Estimate the Average Treatment Effect on the Treated (ATT) of speed governor introduction on city-level speeding rates, using cities that have not yet adopted as controls. Complement with synthetic control methods for the largest cities (Seoul, Busan, Daejeon).
- **Data needed:** `routes_all.parquet` (44.1M trips with timestamps and modes), city-level mode availability dates (derivable from first TUB/ECO trip per city). All data already available.
- **Expected finding:** Speed governor introduction causes a discrete 15-25 percentage point drop in city-level speeding rate, with the effect persisting (no adaptation/rebound) over 6+ months. This would provide *causal* rather than associational evidence for the TUB OR=83-126 finding.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Research Part C or Transportation Science (causal inference in transportation is a growing area, and the Nature Energy micromobility natural experiment paper shows high venue appetite for this design).
- **Connection to current work:** The current paper establishes the associational relationship between modes and speeding. A follow-up providing causal identification would be a natural companion paper, and the dataset is already in hand.

### 2. **Transformer-Based Trip Risk Classification from Raw Speed Sequences** (Feasibility: HIGH)

- **Method:** Train a 1D Transformer (or Mamba state-space model) on the raw per-trip speed vectors to classify trips as safe/risky (binary) or into the 4 GMM classes. Use the LightGBM SHAP analysis as a feature-importance baseline. Compare attention patterns with SHAP feature importance to see whether the Transformer learns the same risk factors. Apply to the full 44.1M trip corpus (the 200K curvature subsample provides labeled ground truth with road geometry).
- **Data needed:** Raw speed arrays from `routes_all.parquet` (already available), GMM cluster labels from Phase 5 (already available). Variable-length sequences can be handled via padding or learned positional encodings.
- **Expected finding:** The Transformer achieves AUC > 0.92 (exceeding LightGBM's 0.905) and attention heads learn to focus on the "ramp-up" and "cruise" phases of trips identified in Phase 2. This validates the hand-crafted indicator approach while providing a more flexible predictor for real-time deployment.
- **Target venue:** NeurIPS or ICML Workshop on ML for Transportation, or TR Part C (aligns with PI's expertise in deep generative models and the TrajGAIL work).
- **Connection to current work:** Directly extends the SHAP analysis (Phase 8.4) by replacing the tabular feature approach with end-to-end sequence modeling. The 44.1M speed profiles are an underexploited asset -- no prior work has trained deep models on e-scooter speed sequences at this scale.

### 3. **Spatial Spillover Analysis of Speed Governor Zones Using Spatial Durbin Models** (Feasibility: MEDIUM)

- **Method:** Apply a Spatial Durbin Error Model (SDEM) to the H3 hex-level speeding rates, testing whether geofenced speed-reduction zones create spatial spillovers -- i.e., whether riders who slow down in a governed zone continue to ride more slowly in adjacent ungoverned zones. Use the existing Gi* hot/cold spot classification as the dependent variable and construct spatial weight matrices from H3 hex adjacency. The 24-month panel enables a spatial panel model (spatial fixed effects + time fixed effects) to control for unobserved hex-level characteristics.
- **Data needed:** `data_parquet/spatial/*.parquet` (H3 hex aggregates, already computed), `data_parquet/trip_h3_indices.parquet`, temporal aggregation to monthly panels (needs computation but straightforward). OSM networks already available for 50 cities.
- **Expected finding:** Positive spatial spillover of 0.1-0.3 (rho parameter), meaning speed reduction in governed zones propagates to neighboring hexes at 10-30% of the direct effect. If confirmed, this has major policy implications -- geofencing works beyond its literal boundaries.
- **Target venue:** Transportation Research Part C or Journal of Transport Geography (spatial econometrics is well-received in these venues; the Moran's I analysis in the current paper establishes spatial autocorrelation as a foundation).
- **Connection to current work:** Builds directly on the Phase 4 spatial analysis (Global Moran's I, Gi* hotspots) by moving from descriptive spatial statistics to a spatial causal model. The existing hot/cold spot infrastructure overlay (hot spots have lower cycling infra) provides the spatial heterogeneity needed for a Spatial Durbin specification.
