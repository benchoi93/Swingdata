\begin{abstract}
Software speed governors are the most widely deployed technological countermeasure for e-scooter speeding, yet asking whether they ``reduce speeding'' is tautological---a device that electronically caps speed mechanically prevents threshold exceedance by design. The more consequential questions concern whether governance reshapes riding behavior on \textit{unconstrained} margins: do riders compensate through more aggressive acceleration, more erratic speed profiles, or altered trip patterns? Does governance reduce ridership or cause platform abandonment? We address these questions using 21 million GPS-instrumented e-scooter trips from 52 South Korean cities (February--November 2023), analyzing eight genuinely behavioral outcomes organized into three categories---safety (harsh acceleration/deceleration, speed variability), demand (trip frequency, distance, user retention), and riding dynamics (cruising fraction, zero-speed fraction)---while treating speed reduction as a mechanical manipulation check. Four analytical blocks converge. Cross-sectional behavioral profiling reveals how governed and ungoverned modes differ across all outcome categories beyond the speed dimension. A natural experiment---the operator's system-wide removal of the ungoverned mode in December 2023---provides causal identification via multi-outcome difference-in-differences with continuous treatment intensity and Bonferroni correction, isolating which behavioral margins respond to governance and which do not. A within-user compensation test on all margins examines whether riders adjust on any unconstrained dimension after losing access to the ungoverned mode. Survival analysis with multiple event types (first harsh event, first high-variability trip, platform churn) quantifies how ungoverned mode availability accelerates behavioral escalation. Together, these analyses provide the first multi-outcome causal evidence on how speed governance reshapes e-scooter riding behavior beyond the governed speed dimension itself, with direct implications for mandatory governor policies and the behavioral cost of speed management.
\end{abstract}

\begin{highlights}
\item Speed governance effects analyzed on genuinely behavioral margins---not the tautological speed reduction
\item Eight outcomes across safety (harsh events, variability), demand (trips, retention), and riding dynamics
\item Natural experiment (ungoverned mode removal) enables causal multi-outcome identification with Bonferroni correction
\item Within-user compensation test on all unconstrained margins using mode-switcher design
\item Multi-endpoint survival analysis including platform churn as a demand-side behavioral response
\end{highlights}
